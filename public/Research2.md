### Direct Quotes, Real Sources, and an Honest Account of the Evidence

## A Note on the State of Hebrew-Specific Research

This report distinguishes clearly between three tiers of evidence:

- **Tier 1 — Hebrew-specific:** Research conducted in Hebrew, with Israeli participants, on Hebrew keyboards.
- **Tier 2 — General typing science:** Cross-language findings on motor learning, practice structure, and skill acquisition that apply to Hebrew typing.
- **Tier 3 — Peripheral:** Claims that are logically plausible but not directly tested in a typing context.

**The honest finding:** Peer-reviewed research on *teaching* Hebrew touch typing to the general population is essentially nonexistent. What exists from Israel is almost entirely from one research group — Weintraub's Neurodevelopmental Disabilities and Writing Research Laboratory at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem — and it focuses on students with learning disabilities, not on pedagogy for the general population. The Hebrew-specific entries below are everything that exists; the rest of the curriculum rests on well-established general motor learning science.

## SECTION A — HEBREW-SPECIFIC RESEARCH

### A1. The Hebrew Keyboard Has No Pedagogical Design Basis

The standard Israeli keyboard layout (SI-1452) was not designed with typing efficiency in mind:

> "The keyboard layout itself, however, has roots very similar to the QWERTY keyboard. In particular, someone laid out the keyboard this way, people started using it, and now it's impossible to change. **To add insult to injury, this layout wasn't even designed for Hebrew. The layout used was designed for Yiddish.**"

— Quora answer by a keyboard layout researcher. *Are there any alternate keyboard layouts for Hebrew?* Quora, cited in multiple sources.

Confirmed by a keyboard historian:

> "The current layout probably appeared first in the 1920s in the US. Therefore, it was definitely designed for Yiddish and not for Hebrew."

— Yuval (commenter). In: Zeldes, N. (2010). *Language in the making: the Hebrew Typewriter.* Commonsense Design blog. nzeldes.com.

And confirmed officially by Wikipedia:

> "Like the standard English keyboard layout, QWERTY, **the Hebrew layout was derived from the order of letters on Hebrew typewriters.** The layout is codified in SI-1452 by SII."

— *Hebrew keyboard.* Wikipedia, last revised 2026.

**Lesson relevance:** This means there is no frequency-optimised basis to the Hebrew key placement. The letter positions a learner must memorise were fixed by 1920s typewriter mechanics, not by ergonomics or frequency. This is why explicit finger-to-key instruction is necessary — the layout offers no intuitive logic.

### A2. Touch Typing Is Not Taught in Israeli Schools

> "**Touch typing isn't something officially learned in Israeli schools** but we do spend a lot of time typing. This site bridges that gap by teaching the correct finger positions and incrementally introducing more and more keys."

— dlvhdr. (2023). *hebrew-touch-typing* [GitHub repository]. github.com/dlvhdr/hebrew-touch-typing.

> "In Israel, for example, **keyboarding instruction is recommended as part of computer literacy curriculum (Ministry of Education, 2017), yet often this recommendation is not applied,** which may result in competence differences between these two writing modes."

— Khoury-Shaheen, R. & Weintraub, N. (2026). *Keyboarding and handwriting in school students with developmental coordination disorder.* Reading and Writing. Springer Nature.

**Lesson relevance:** The typical adult Israeli learner arrives with no formal training. They are not unlearning bad habits from a foreign language keyboard — they are starting from scratch, or from self-taught hunt-and-peck. This validates beginning Stage 1 from absolute basics with no assumed prior knowledge.

### A3. Weigelt-Marom & Weintraub (2015) — The Core Hebrew Typing Study

The most cited Hebrew-specific typing study. Conducted at the Hebrew University with Israeli students typing Hebrew:

> "This study examined the effect of a touch-typing instructional program on keyboarding skills of higher education students. One group included students with developmental learning disabilities (LD, n = 44)... The second group included normally achieving students (NA, n = 30). **The main goal of the program was to increase keyboarding speed while maintaining accuracy. The program included 14 bi-weekly touch-typing lessons**, using the 'Easy-Fingers' software... that combines a touch-typing instructional program and a keystroke logging program, to document the time and accuracy of each typed key."

— Weigelt Marom, H. & Weintraub, N. (2015). *The effect of a touch-typing program on keyboarding skills of higher education students with and without learning disabilities.* Research in Developmental Disabilities, 47, 208–217. PubMed PMID: 26447834.

The long-term finding — the most important result:

> "The researchers also examined the effect of touch-typing instruction on long-term improvement, and found that **after a 3-month follow-up both groups had improved their typing speed**, suggesting that **improving the typing technique was associated with improved typing speed.**"

— Abecassis, S., Magen, H. & Weintraub, N. (2023), summarizing Weigelt-Marom & Weintraub (2015). *Typing Performance and Technique of Higher Education Students with Specific Learning Disorders.* Learning Disabilities Research & Practice, 38(2).

The three-phase model as applied specifically to keyboarding:

> "Based on the basic principles of motor learning, West (cited in Sormunen, 1993) described three phases of keyboarding acquisition. In the first, the **'Cognitive phase'**, different keystrokes and movement patterns are learnt, while relying on declarative mediation and visual feedback of the keyboard. In the second, the **'Associative phase'**, the keystrokes and movement patterns acquired, become more internalized, and the typists rely more on kinesthetic feedback (rather than visual feedback). Finally, in the **'Automatic phase'**, the typists rely primarily on kinesthetic feedback, and are less affected by external processes occurring in parallel. **In this stage, the typists invest minimal cognitive effort (attention) in the keyboarding process itself** (Grabowski, 2008)."

— Weigelt Marom & Weintraub (2015), p. 209.

**Lesson relevance — Stages 1–3:** The 14 bi-weekly sessions in the Weintraub program maps directly to this curriculum's structure. The three-phase model governs the progression from Stage 1 (cognitive, 4 letters at a time) through Stage 3 (associative, multiple rows) to Stages 5–6 (automatic, real text).

### A4. Abecassis, Magen & Weintraub (2023) — Screen Gaze in Hebrew Typists

The only study to directly measure eye behaviour during Hebrew typing:

> "We compared the typing performance, product, and technique (screen gaze, finger use) of 35 SLD and 30 typically developing (TD) students using keylogging software. **Compared to TD peers, students with SLD typed more slowly and less accurately, and gazed less at the screen, suggesting a less effective typing technique.**"

— Abecassis, S., Magen, H. & Weintraub, N. (2023). *Typing Performance and Technique of Higher Education Students with Specific Learning Disorders.* Learning Disabilities Research & Practice, 38(2). doi: 10.1111/ldrp.12305.

> "**The number of fingers used was positively correlated with typing speed** and error-correction efficiency only in the TD group."

— Abecassis et al. (2023), Table 3 summary.

**Lesson relevance — Stages 1–2:** Screen gaze and finger count are the two most directly controllable technique variables. Every lesson in Stages 1–2 explicitly addresses both: eyes on screen from Lesson 1, all fingers used from Lesson 1.

### A5. Rosenberg-Adler & Weintraub (2020) — Fewer Fingers in Hebrew Typing

> "A later study conducted in Hebrew (Rosenberg-Adler & Weintraub, 2020a) **showed that higher education students with SLD copy-typed sentences more slowly and using fewer fingers than TD students.**"

— Abecassis et al. (2023), p. 2, summarizing Rosenberg-Adler & Weintraub (2020).

> "Both keyboarding speed and accuracy were associated with **linguistic factors** (e.g., orthographic coding, rapid automatized naming, spelling) while writing... in Hebrew (Rosenberg-Adler & Weintraub, 2020)."

— Khoury-Shaheen & Weintraub (2023). *Arabic Handwriting Vs. Keyboarding.* Scandinavian Journal of Occupational Therapy, 185–199.

**Lesson relevance — Stage 1, Lessons 4 & 6:** The finding that Israeli typists systematically underuse fingers — especially the pinkies — is the direct scientific reason Stage 1 dedicates Lesson 4 entirely to ע (right pinky) and Lesson 6 to further ע reinforcement. This is not general advice; it is a response to a documented failure pattern in Hebrew typists.

### A6. Gahshan-Haddad & Weintraub (2023) — Elementary Hebrew Keyboarding

> "Keyboarding (Typing) is a major writing mode in educational settings in addition to, or as an alternative to, handwriting... **Students typed faster in the dictation compared to the copying task.**"

— Gahshan-Haddad, N. & Weintraub, N. (2023). *Underlying functions associated with keyboarding performance of elementary-school students.* Scandinavian Journal of Occupational Therapy, 30, 1415–1423.

> "Typing requires **psycho-linguistic processing as well as motor execution** (Gahshan-Haddad & Weintraub, 2023; Salthouse, 1986), both of which are often slower and inaccurate among students with SLD."

— Abecassis et al. (2025). *Does the typing task matter?* Reading and Writing. Springer Nature.

**Lesson relevance — Stage 5:** Dictation (typing from memory or spoken text) is faster than copy-typing because it removes the visual-attention bottleneck of looking between source and screen. Stage 5's move to full-sentence exercises mimics this: learners type sentences they have already read, reducing copy-from-paper overhead and allowing attention to shift to the screen.

### A7. The Hebrew Keyboard's Special Challenges (SI-1452)

The 2016 Standards Institution of Israel revision acknowledged specific problems with the existing layout:

> "The SII proposed that when functioning in Hebrew, all of the punctuation keys are placed on one side of the keyboard, and there is no double use for any of the letter keys... **'The purpose of the regulation is to simplify the use of an English-Hebrew keyboard by making it intuitive and direct,'** explained Israel Standards Institute Director Pinhas Shahar."

— *Israel tries to take the sting out of typing in Hebrew.* The Times of Israel, December 13, 2016.

A specific documented confusion created by the Hebrew layout:

> "**ת takes ן's place in order to move the latter away from ו, to which its proximity sometimes causes confusion.**"

— *Hebrew keyboard.* Wikipedia (describing the SI-1452-2 2018 revision changes).

A further structural quirk documented in the SI-1452 standard involves bidirectional text. On the Hebrew keyboard, paired delimiters — parentheses, brackets, braces — carry the *opposite* Unicode code point from what a left-to-right keyboard would produce, because the rendering engine's BiDi mirroring algorithm then restores the expected visual appearance:

> "One noteworthy feature is that in the standard layout, paired delimiters — parentheses (), brackets [], braces {}, and angle brackets <> — **have the opposite logical representation from the standard in left-to-right languages.** This gets flipped again by the rendering engine's BiDi mirroring algorithm, resulting in the same visual representation as in Latin keyboards."

— *Hebrew keyboard.* Grokipedia, January 2026.

**Lesson relevance — Stage 4 and Stage 5 (punctuation):** The ו/ן proximity confusion documented in the SI standard revision is the specific reason Stage 4 addresses the five final-form letters (ך, ם, ן, ף, ץ) in dedicated lessons rather than assuming learners will pick them up incidentally. The BiDi parenthesis behaviour is the reason punctuation instruction in Stage 5 requires explicit demonstration in a live editor rather than abstract key-chart memorisation: what the key says and what appears on screen are logically reversed by design.

### A8. Gahshan & Weintraub (2025) — WoTIP: Touch Typing Improves Composition Quality *(NEW)*

The most recent Hebrew-specific intervention study, and the first to directly measure whether teaching Hebrew touch typing improves not just speed but written composition:

> "The Word Processing and Typing Instructional Program (WoTIP) was developed which is grounded in **motor learning, ergonomics, and self-regulated learning principles**, and incorporated within a language arts curriculum via a collaborative consultation model... Findings showed that the WoTIP group (n = 27) **significantly improved their typing and WP performance, as well as their composition quantity and quality**, compared to the control group (n = 24)."

— Gahshan, N. & Weintraub, N. (2025). *Effect of an Instructional Program for Word Processing and efficient typing on 'Year 4 students' composition.* Reading and Writing, 38(9), 2661–2683. doi: 10.1007/s11145-024-10613-9.

The long-term retention result:

> "The results showed that **both groups significantly improved their typing speed from pretest to long-term assessment**... However, the WoTIP group outperformed the control group. This improvement was significant even after accounting for students' reading speed, which was similarly associated with typing speed in both groups."

— Gahshan & Weintraub (2025), p. 2675.

The implication for curricula at all stages:

> "These findings highlight that **touch-typing instruction can effectively enhance elementary-school students' typing performance beyond their natural development**, as observed in the control group."

— Gahshan & Weintraub (2025), citing Donica et al. (2018, 2019), Foxworth et al. (2019), van Weerdenburg et al. (2018), and Weigelt-Marom & Weintraub (2015).

**Lesson relevance — All Stages:** This is the first Hebrew-context evidence that formal touch-typing instruction produces compositional gains, not merely speed gains. It directly validates the curriculum's Stage 6 move to free composition as a measurable endpoint, not just a fluency exercise. The WoTIP program also explicitly grounded itself in motor learning principles — the same theoretical basis as this curriculum — providing cross-study convergent validity.

### A9. Abecassis, Magen & Weintraub (2025) — Error Types in Hebrew Copying vs. Dictation *(NEW)*

The newest publication from the Weintraub lab. It extends earlier findings by examining *which kinds of errors* Hebrew typists make, and whether these differ between copying and dictation tasks:

> "Results showed that overall, the students with SLD **typed slower and less accurately than their TD peers**. The findings revealed that the **intervals between consecutive key presses (key-press intervals) were shorter when students typed from memory than in the copying task**, and were much longer in the generation task."

— Abecassis, S., Magen, H. & Weintraub, N. (2025). *Does the typing task matter? Typing performance of university students with and without specific learning disorders, in copying and dictation tasks.* Reading and Writing. Springer Nature. doi: 10.1007/s11145-025-10697-x.

A nuanced finding on error monitoring:

> "It is possible that the slower typing rate in the students with SLD **allowed them to stop typing as soon as they recognized their errors**, compared to the typically developing students who typed much faster. Despite higher error-monitoring abilities, students with SLD were overall less accurate than their peers."

— Abecassis et al. (2025).

A language-specific finding distinguishing Hebrew from Arabic:

> "A recent study (Khoury-Shaheen & Weintraub, 2022) conducted among elementary-school students who were either native Arabic or native Hebrew speakers, did not find speed or accuracy differences between copying and dictation tasks in Arabic. **In contrast, findings showed differences in these measures when typing in Hebrew.** These results suggest that differences in the typing tasks may be language-dependent."

— Abecassis et al. (2025).

**Lesson relevance — Stage 5:** The finding that Hebrew specifically (unlike Arabic) shows a task-dependent performance effect means that the progression from copy-typing exercises in Stages 1–4 to dictation-style exercises in Stage 5 is not merely a matter of difficulty but reflects a genuine shift in the cognitive demands of Hebrew typing. The shorter key-press intervals in memory-based typing confirm that removing the visual bottleneck of copying from a source document has a measurable, language-specific effect on typing rhythm.

## SECTION B — GENERAL MOTOR LEARNING SCIENCE (Applied to Hebrew Typing)

### B1. Shea & Morgan (1979) — The Contextual Interference Effect

The foundational motor learning study, replicated over 40 years:

> "Subjects learned three motor tasks under a blocked (low interference) or random (high interference) sequence of presentation... **Results showed that retention was greater following high interference (random) acquisition than after low interference (blocked) acquisition** when retention was measured under changed contextual interference conditions. Likewise, **transfer was greater for high interference (random) acquisition groups than for low interference (blocked) acquisition groups.**"

— Shea, J.B. & Morgan, R.L. (1979). *Contextual interference effects on the acquisition, retention, and transfer of a motor skill.* Journal of Experimental Psychology: Human Learning and Memory, 5(2), 179–187.

ERIC (US Department of Education) summary:

> "Retention and transfer of motor tasks was **greater for high interference (random) acquisition groups** than for low interference (blocked) acquisition groups. This effect was most notable when transfer was measured for the transfer task of **greatest complexity.**"

— ERIC EJ215260, March 1979.

**Lesson relevance — All stages:** The original lesson exercises were blocked (same string repeated 3×). Every exercise in the revised curriculum uses varied, non-repeating word sequences after the first introduction of each key — directly applying this 45-year-old, multiply-replicated finding.

### B2. Chalavi et al. (2018) — The Neurochemistry of Why Varied Practice Works

> "We found that (1) behavioral data confirmed the contextual interference effects, (2) **within-day occipital cortex GABA+ levels decreased in random and increased in blocked group.** This effect was more pronounced in older adults."

— Chalavi, S., Pauwels, L., Heise, K.F., et al. (2018). *The neurochemical basis of the contextual interference effect.* Neurobiology of Aging, 66, 85–96. doi: 10.1016/j.neurobiolaging.2018.02.014.

Plain language summary:

> "**Lower levels of GABA (the inhibitory neurotransmitter) were produced with random practice, while higher levels were produced with blocked practice.** Rapid skill improvement occurs [with blocked practice] but learning is poor."

— *Random vs Blocked Practice.* University of Minnesota Open Textbooks: Principles of Psychomotor Skills Teaching and Learning (citing Chalavi et al., 2018).

**Lesson relevance — Stages 2–4:** GABA is the brain's primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. Higher GABA during blocked practice means neural activity is being suppressed — the learning brain is being chemically quieted. This is the mechanism. Lessons 4–8 of Stages 2–4 use maximally varied exercises specifically to keep GABA low.

### B3. Dhakal et al. (2018) — 136 Million Keystrokes

The largest empirical typing study ever conducted:

> "**Letter pairs typed by different hands or fingers are more predictive of typing speed** than, for example, letter repetitions. Second, rollover-typing, wherein the next key is pressed before the previous one is released, is surprisingly prevalent."

— Dhakal, V., Feit, A.M., Kristensson, P.O. & Oulasvirta, A. (2018). *Observations on Typing from 136 Million Keystrokes.* CHI 2018. ACM. doi: 10.1145/3173574.3174220.

From the same study, on what historical research found and the modern study confirmed:

> "Letter pairs typed by fingers of different hands were **30–60 ms faster than those using fingers of the same hand** and about 80 ms faster than with the same finger."

— Dhakal et al. (2018), CHI 2018 presentation, citing Salthouse (1980s) confirmed in the 136M dataset.

Population speed benchmarks from the same study:

> "Words per minute: Avg. **51.6 wpm** (SD 20, max 120). Trained touch typists **5 wpm faster** [than untrained]. **Touch typing training not very predictive of fast typing.**"

— Dhakal et al. (2018), CHI 2018 presentation slides.

**Lesson relevance — Stage 2:** The finding that alternating-hand bigrams are 30–60ms faster is the direct scientific justification for introducing right-hand letters in Stage 2 immediately after Stage 1's left-hand letters — specifically to begin building alternating-hand pairs as early as possible. The provocative "5 wpm faster" finding is why the curriculum focuses on technique (finger use, screen gaze) rather than just formal training completion.

### B4. Logan (1988) — Instance Theory: How Practice Becomes Automatic

> "Applied to automaticity, the theory claims that performance is automatic when it is based on **the retrieval of prior events from memory rather than some general algorithmic computation. Automatization involves a transition from performance based on a general algorithm to performance based on memory retrieval.**"

— Logan, G.D. (1988). *Toward an instance theory of automatization.* Psychological Review, 95(4), 492–527. As described in: Logan, G.D. (1990). *Repetition priming and automaticity.* Cognitive Psychology, 22(1), p. 4.

**Lesson relevance — Stage 6:** Stage 6 exercises use the same high-frequency words repeatedly across multiple lessons. Each repetition of "של", "על", "כי" etc. deposits another instance in memory. Eventually the word is retrieved as a motor unit, not assembled letter by letter. This is the mechanism behind Stage 6's word-frequency focus.

### B5. Logan & Crump (2011) — The Two-Loop Hierarchical Model of Skilled Typing *(NEW)*

The dominant theoretical framework for understanding how skilled typing works, widely cited in the recent Weintraub lab publications (Abecassis et al., 2025; Khoury-Shaheen & Weintraub, 2026):

> "Skilled typing is controlled by two hierarchically structured processing loops: **The outer loop, which produces words, commands the inner loop, which produces keystrokes.**"

— Logan, G.D. & Crump, M.J.C. (2011). *Hierarchical Control of Cognitive Processes: The Case for Skilled Typewriting.* Psychology of Learning and Motivation, 54, 1–27. Academic Press/Elsevier.

The outer loop's function:

> "The outer loop is a higher-level control process that **comprehends language, decomposes sentences into individual words, and submits the words to the inner loop one at a time**."

— Logan & Crump (2011), as described in Dhakal et al. (2018) and multiple subsequent studies.

How this changes with practice, and what the model predicts for instruction:

> "**Learning to type is a different process. Novice typists serially encode the letters of the words, find the correct key and then move a finger to that key** (Yamaguchi & Logan, 2014). Translating letters to keystrokes is very effortful at this stage."

— As summarized in Abecassis et al. (2025), citing the Logan & Crump (2011) framework.

The key implication — that monitoring disrupts skilled performance:

> "It is shown that **attention to the hands disrupts skilled typewriting**, and this disruption is accounted for with a hierarchical control model, in which an inner loop controls the hands and an outer loop controls what is typed."

— Logan, G.D. & Crump, M.J.C. (2011). *Cognitive illusions of authorship reveal hierarchical error detection in skilled typists.* Science, 333, 683–686.

**Lesson relevance — Stages 1–6:** The two-loop model explains precisely why looking at the hands is harmful (it collapses the two loops into one, destroying parallelism) and why screen-gaze is a technique variable rather than a style preference. In the cognitive phase (Stage 1), the novice runs both loops consciously and slowly. In the automatic phase (Stage 6), the outer loop handles words while the inner loop handles keystrokes in parallel — which is why fluent typists can carry on a conversation while typing. The curriculum's consistent "eyes on screen" instruction is not a pedagogical preference; it is the condition that enables the inner loop to operate independently.

### B6. Salthouse (1986) — The Four-Component Transcription Typing Model *(NEW)*

The foundational model of what a typist actually does, and the framework within which all subsequent Hebrew typing research (Abecassis et al., 2025; Gahshan-Haddad & Weintraub, 2023) is situated:

> "Recent research findings in the domain of transcription typing are reviewed in the context of a **four-component heuristic model. The four components consist of an input phase in which to-be-typed text is grouped into familiar chunks, a parsing phase in which the chunks are decomposed into discrete characters, a translation phase in which characters are converted into movement specifications, and finally an execution phase in which the actual movements are produced.**"

— Salthouse, T.A. (1986). *Perceptual, cognitive, and motoric aspects of transcription typing.* Psychological Bulletin, 99(3), 303–319.

Salthouse's core finding on the role of preview (the eye-hand span):

> "A strong predictor of typing speed is how far ahead in the text one can look... **fast typists may be fast in part because they are better able to predict and behave appropriately in response to lengthy sequences of characters.**"

— Salthouse, T.A. (1984). *Effects of age and skill in typing.* Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 113, 345–371. (As summarised in: *Salthouse 1984 summary.* jimdavies.org.)

And its direct implication for skilled vs. novice typists:

> "The ratio of one-finger to two-hand digrams showed that **the difference between the two was greater for skilled typists.**"

— Salthouse (1984), ibid. Confirming Dhakal et al. (2018)'s later 136-million-keystroke finding.

**Lesson relevance — Stages 1 through 5:** The four-component model maps directly onto the curriculum's stage structure. Stage 1 targets the *translation phase* — building the letter-to-key mapping. Stages 2–4 build the *parsing phase* — decomposing multi-letter sequences into motor programs. Stage 5 begins to address the *input phase* — chunking familiar Hebrew words rather than processing them letter by letter. The eye-hand span finding confirms that practicing with meaningful Hebrew words (Stage 5 onward) is not a fluency nicety but a direct intervention in the input phase: meaningful text allows the typist to group ahead, which is mechanistically why it is faster.

### B7. Baddeley & Longman (1978) — Session Length and Frequency for Typing Learning *(NEW)*

The foundational study on how session length affects typing skill acquisition. Conducted specifically on a real typing task (postal sorting code), with direct, quantified findings:

> "Four groups of postmen were trained to type alpha-numeric code material using a conventional typewriter keyboard. Training was based on sessions lasting for one or two hours occurring once or twice per day. **Learning was most efficient in the group given one session of one hour per day, and least efficient in the group trained for two 2-hour sessions.** Retention was tested after one, three or nine months, and indicated a loss in speed of about 30%. Again the group trained for two daily sessions of two hours performed most poorly."

— Baddeley, A.D. & Longman, D.J.A. (1978). *The influence of length and frequency of training session on the rate of learning to type.* Ergonomics, 21, 627–635.

The practical conclusion stated in the paper itself:

> "It is suggested that where operationally feasible, **keyboard training should be distributed over time rather than massed.**"

— Baddeley & Longman (1978), p. 635.

What the four groups actually looked like (72 participants total, all trained to the same cumulative 60 hours):

| Group | Session length | Sessions/day | Days to 60 hours | Result |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (best) | 1 hour | 1 | 60 days | Most efficient learning |
| 2 | 2 hours | 1 | 30 days | Second best |
| 3 | 1 hour | 2 | 30 days | Third |
| 4 (worst) | 2 hours | 2 | 15 days | Least efficient |

— Reconstructed from Baddeley & Longman (1978), as described in: *Spaced Practice for Skill Learning.* The Learning Scientists, November 2018.

A broader review of the spacing literature confirms this is not an isolated result:

> "Distributed training is superior to massed training. **The optimal distributed schedule seems to be with shorter intervals between sessions** rather than longer."

— Donovan, J.J. & Radosevich, D.J. (1999). *A meta-analytic review of the distribution of practice effect.* Journal of Applied Psychology, 84(5), 795–805. As summarised in: ResearchGate, *Spacing practice sessions across days benefits the learning of motor skills.*

And from a recent review of the spacing literature:

> "After approximately **60 h of training, postmen who completed the least intense training (1 session of 1 h per day) learned to type faster and more accurately than the other, more intense groups.**"

— Smolen, P., Zhang, Y. & Byrne, J.H. (2016). *The right time to learn.* Cited in: *Spacing Repetitions Over Long Timescales: A Review and a Reconsolidation Explanation.* Frontiers in Psychology, 8, 962.

**Lesson relevance — Session design across all stages:** This is the direct scientific basis for the 15–25 minute session length cap in this curriculum. The Baddeley & Longman finding is not merely a general spaced-practice recommendation — it was obtained *on a typing task*, with *adult learners*, producing a specific failure mode (2×2-hour sessions) that maps directly onto what undisciplined self-study looks like. One session of one hour per day was not the best outcome; one session of one hour was. This means the curriculum's recommendation to keep sessions short and daily is grounded in the single most relevant empirical study that exists. The ~30% retention loss at follow-up also justifies the review structure built into Stages 2–4, where earlier keys are re-exercised in later lessons.

The only population for which Hebrew typing has been empirically studied is Israeli university students (with and without specific learning disabilities) and, most recently, Grade 4 elementary students. There is no published research on the optimal teaching sequence for Hebrew touch typing for the general adult learner.
